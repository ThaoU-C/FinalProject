Kelp need your help!

California kelp forests, a habitat for more than 800 marine species, are dying because of climate change

90% was killed off between 2014 and 2016
75% reduced over the past century

“The California coast without kelp is like the Amazon without trees,”
states by Tom Ford, an executive director of the Bay Foundation.

The kelp forest ecosystem along the coast of California provides many benefits. It absorbs CO2 and reduces the acidification of the ocean that would be a threat to marine life. Through photosynthesis, kelp increase the oxygen levels in the ocean and control erosion by reducing the speed and size of waves impacting the shore. Kelp can also be used as a source of biofuel. Lastly, around 800 species of marine life depend on the kelp forest to survive. Marine mammals use kelp forests not only as feeding grounds but also as places to hide from their predators.
However, kelp forests are dying. A heat wave between 2014 and 2016 killed off 90% of kelp living along the northern California coast. And, in southern California, kelp has already been reduced by 75% in some areas. Because overfishing and pollution reduced the predators and competitors of the purple sea urchin, its population has exploded and consumed vast tracts of kelp forests, leaving a barren seascape in their wake.

More details

Sea Otter

Southern sea otters live along California’s central coast, from San Mateo County to Santa Barbara. The sea otter is a keystone species since its health is an indicator of the overall health of the habitat and environment. Sea otters help maintain the health of kelp forests by eating sea urchins and controlling their population and preventing the emergence of urchin barrens, which are areas devoid of any kelp. The fur coat of sea otters is the densest in the world, made up of more than 1 million hairs per square inch, compared to just 100,000 hairs per square inch that people have.

Sea otters has been listed as endangered species under the Endangered Species Act since 1977. The California population averages around only 3,000 individuals.

Seal

Harbor seals are one of the most common marine mammals along the U.S. West and East Coasts. They weigh up to 285 pounds and measure up to 6 feet in length. They are protected under the Marine Mammal Protection Act. They have short forelimbs, or flippers. They also lack external ear flaps and instead have a small hole on either side of their head.

Harbor seals are facing Habitat Degradation. Oil and gas development, commercial and recreational development (including resort development), and increased vessel traffic may displace seals or their prey that would normally use those areas. They accumulate contaminants, which threaten their immune and reproductive systems, in their blubber, blood, and organs.

Sea Cow

Sea cows used to live along the coast of California and became extinct in the 18th Century, just 27 years after first being discovered by Europeans. Scientists theorize that sea cows would have kept kelp forests healthy by grazing kelp at the surface and allowing sunlight to reach deeper into the water and allow algae to grow and become a food source for sea urchins. Sea Lion

Sea lion habitat range from southeast Alaska to central Mexico. The sea lion population is considered least concerned since it is estimated to be up to 300,000 individuals.

However, sea lions are affected by climate change since warm waters can reduce plant abundance, which will affect the population of the prey that they consume and cascade up to higher levels in the food chain where sea lions reside. Warming waters can also cause algal blooms, which produce a toxin known as domoic acid and can attack the brain, causing seizures and disorientation. This toxin will accumulate in fish and other marine animals that consume algae. Eventually, the toxin will reach sea lions when they prey on marine animals.

Gray Whale

Gray whales are migratory and the North Pacific population’s habitat range from the Bering and Chukchi seas to Baja California in Mexico. The gray whale population was estimated to be 27,000 in 2016 but has recently crashed to 16,650 in the winter of 2021 and 2022. Scientists believe that recent retreating of sea ice and warming waters are causing a reduction of the food that gray whales eat and causing many gray whales to be malnourished and die.

Other species

Besides many marine mammals, other marine species like fishes, seashells, and seabirds also depend on the kelp forests to survive. The disappearance of kelp forests is a great threat that requires immediate actions because it will affect marine life and our resources.

HOW TO HELP?

Bay Foundation. is hiring divers to find and kill purple sea urchins. Once the urchins have been removed, the nonprofit reseed the area with abalone to help attract other marine animals.
Sustainable Surf engages the surfing community to finance the SeaTrees program to restore kelp or remove purple sea urchins.
Urchinomics is testing a method to ranch purple sea urchin by catching them from sea barrens and raising them in tanks to sell to sushi or seafood restaurants.
The University of Southern California is researching techniques to farm kelp efficiently to use as biofuel and sequester carbon to offset emissions.
The California Ocean Protection Council is planning to create a string of kelp refuge along the coast that can seed surrounding areas and help regrow kelp. But it is a race against time as people and kelp fight for the future of a healthy and vibrant kelp forest ecosystem.

And, many other scientists are working hard to restore California's kelp forests.
